Overview of the Proposal

The meeting addressed a developer's request to rezone a 35-acre parcel from A1 (Agricultural) to R1 (Residential) to allow for a development of up to 78 homes on one-third-acre lots. The developer, represented by Brad Brown, has a conditional purchase agreement with the property owner, Jeff Gibson. The hearing was held to gather public comment on the proposal.

Concerns & Proposed Solutions from Residents

An overwhelming majority of residents who spoke were strongly opposed to the R1 rezone proposal. Their concerns were multifaceted and often repeated by multiple speakers.

Opposition to R-1 Zoning & Density

  • Contradicts Community Character: The primary concern was that a high-density, third-acre lot subdivision fundamentally contradicts Millville's identity as a town of "Country Livin' at its Finest." Speakers emphasized that they and others moved to Millville specifically to escape suburban sprawl and enjoy open spaces. (Riley Peterson, Ken Hamilton, Cole Thornton, Mandie Bagley, Susan Goldberry, Charlotte McConkie, Lynda Hendrickson)
  • Loss of Open Space and Farmland: Residents described the South Fields as a "treasure" and an essential part of the town's ambiance, used for recreation, enjoyment of views, and wildlife habitat. Many speakers lamented the loss of productive farmland. (Jim Christensen, Jeanette Paulsen, Ken Hamilton, Linda Hendrickson)
  • Negative Precedent: A decision to allow this rezone would set a precedent, likely leading other landowners in the South Fields to sell for similar high-density developments, irreversibly changing the entire area. (Jeff David)

Adherence to the General Plan

  • Violation of Existing Plan: Several speakers noted that the proposal violates the current General Plan, which calls for large-lot development to preserve the rural atmosphere. They argued that even if the plan is old, it is the legally binding document that should guide development until it is formally and publicly updated. (Crystal Zimmerman, Cammy Raymon, Luanne Pierson)
  • Removal of Previous Protections: Residents expressed distrust over the council's recent removal of a city code clause that required two-acre lots for any rezone south of 400 South, feeling it was a "betrayal of trust" that paved the way for this proposal. (Charlotte McConkie)

Infrastructure & Services

  • Water Scarcity: This was a major data-driven concern. Residents cited Utah's drought conditions and presented calculations suggesting the 11 water shares associated with the property are insufficient.
  • Jared Nichols calculated that the 78 proposed homes would require 26 million gallons annually, while the shares provide only 19 million, creating a 7-million-gallon deficit.
  • Celeste Thatcher reiterated these numbers and noted that Millville is in a "severe drought" and has had historical issues with nitrate levels in one of its wells.
  • Sewer System Capacity: The city's new sewer system is untested. Residents questioned its capacity to handle 78 new homes, especially given the existing pipe sizes in certain areas. (Jeanette Paulsen, Kim Ashcroft, Albert Monson)
  • School Overcrowding: The local elementary school is already at capacity. Adding potentially 50+ new cars for drop-off and pick-up would create a "nightmare" and strain educational resources. (Chloe Baker, Kim Ashcroft)
  • Increased Traffic: Residents, particularly those on Canyon Road and near the South Fields, are concerned about increased traffic, speeding, and recreational vehicle use on roads not designed for it. (Kady Bird, Chloe Baker)

Financial Concerns & City Governance

  • Residential Homes as a Liability: Multiple speakers argued that residential development is a net financial loss for a city, costing more in services than it generates in property taxes. (Jared Nichols, Luanne Pierson, Chloe Baker)
  • Questioning the City's Motivation: Residents questioned the urgency and financial justification for the rezone. Theo Matthews challenged the council on finances, questioning the salary of city employees when compared to similar positions in much larger communities.
  • Lack of Transparency: Speakers requested more transparency from the council on why this specific development is being considered, what the benefits are, and why alternative revenue sources aren't being prioritized. (Chloe Baker, Peter Thatcher, Jeff David)
  • Timing of the Vote: Garrett Greinghalgh strongly urged the council to vote on the issue before the upcoming election, not after, so that citizens could hold them accountable. She stated, "to say we're gonna have the vote after the election holds nobody's feet to the fire."

Contradiction of Planning & Zoning Commission Recommendation

  • Ignoring Unanimous Vote: It was raised by Theo Matthews that the city's own Planning and Zoning Commission had reviewed the proposal and returned a unanimous vote against it. He questioned the council directly: "Why ignore the unanimous Planning and Zoning Committee vote against the proposal?" This was presented as evidence that the council was potentially acting against the recommendation of its own appointed advisory body and the will of the community.

Proposed Solutions

  • Rezone to R3 (Two-Acre Lots): The most common proposal was to deny the R1 rezone but approve an R3 rezone for two-acre lots, which would be consistent with the recently removed city code and the general sentiment of the community. (Riley Peterson, Jared Nichols, Cammy Raymon)
  • Jared Nichols provided a detailed alternative, stating he had 12 buyers (80% of whom are current Millville residents) ready to purchase 15 two-acre lots, which would generate $330,000 in impact fees and result in a 10-million-gallon water surplus.
  • Focus on Commercial Development: Several residents suggested the city focus its revenue-generating efforts on the commercially zoned property along Highway 165 instead of relying on one-time residential impact fees. (Jeanette Paulsen, Theo Matthews, Mandie Bagley, Charlotte McConkie)
  • Engage an Ombudsman: Jeff David proposed a three-step process involving the state ombudsman to independently verify the city's financial and legal reasoning, hold a transparent public review, and conduct a brief financial audit to ensure decisions are based on verified facts.

Developer Representative's Position (Brad Brown)

Brad Brown, representing the developer, adopted a conciliatory tone.

  • He asked the council to balance the opposition with the needs of the rest of the city. He noted that he had counted 157 people in attendance, representing about 6.9% of Millville's population, and suggested that residents not in attendance were likely not opposed to the development.
  • He also mentioned renters who "would likely want to have a house" and positioned the developer as a potential "good partner" who wants to listen to what matters to citizens and does not want to "disrupt" the special character of the town.

Property Seller's Position (Jeff Gibson)

Jeff Gibson, the owner of the 35-acre parcel, provided his personal perspective.

  • He stated that he has owned the property for a decade and has never previously tried to rezone it. He was approached by three different developers and chose the one he felt most "in harmony with."
  • He noted that the proposed one-third-acre lot size is contiguous with an existing subdivision.
  • He expressed a personal desire to move from Providence to Millville. He explained that after selling the 35-acre parcel, he and his wife discussed purchasing one of the newly created one-third-acre lots to build their home and live in the community, stating, "I would love to be your neighbor."

City Council's Response to Public Comment

The council members listened to the comments before closing the public hearing and offering their individual responses. No decision was made at the meeting.

  • Ryan Zollinger: He thanked the public for their input and acknowledged their concerns. He noted that the council has rejected other high-density projects and is working on the citizens' behalf. He also made several key points:
  • On the General Plan: "You know, the general plan is old." He acknowledged it is a guiding document but noted he was on the council when it was created.
  • On school congestion: He stated that service to the elementary school "is not really Millville city's responsibility. Um, you know that's that's the school's responsibility to to manage that stuff and and deal with the congestion."
  • He emphasized the importance of private property rights. He also stated that he had received messages from residents who support the project and attempted to read one aloud. However, when challenged by the audience to identify the author, he was unable to do so and refrained from sharing the comment.
  • Pamela June: She clarified that the prior code change (removing the two-acre lot requirement) was made on legal advice because the code illegally took zoning power away from the council, and it was done before the developer approached the city. She assured residents the council is not "in the pockets" of developers.
  • Daniel Grange: He stated that for him, "this is not a financial decision." He explained that the city has already zoned land for commercial retail along the highway and has been actively trying to attract tenants, but has been unsuccessful. He assured the audience he had not made a decision and would weigh public comment "very, very heavily."
  • Clay Wilker: He expressed empathy for the residents, stating, "I know what you're fighting for... It's definitely worth fighting for." He acknowledged the "overwhelming sentiment" against the proposal but also noted the council must consider the property rights of other agricultural landowners in the city.
  • Jeremy Ward: He defended the council's process, explaining they are obligated to consider all proposals brought to them and that doing so does not make them the "enemy." He defended the city's transparency, stating everything is posted online. He concluded with a forceful defense of the City Recorder against comments about his salary:
  • "We are lucky to have Corey Tweed. We are very lucky to have him. He works his butt off for the city and I did not appreciate that guy with that crap about him."
  • Mayor David Hair: The mayor concluded by defending the integrity and hard work of the council members, stating they have spent "thousands of hours" and "sleepless nights" on behalf of the city. He expressed disappointment that some might base their election vote on this single issue, calling such a view "shortsighted."
